# Brand Strategy — Discovery Questions & Coaching

Ask one at a time. After each answer, run it against the coaching triggers below; deliver the nudge if one fires, then let them revise or move on. Capture raw answers; polish in synthesis.

## Coaching Triggers

| Trigger | How to detect | Response |
|---------|---------------|----------|
| Too short / shallow | A few words, no texture | A specific follow-up that forces detail |
| Marketing speak | "save time," "empowered," "seamless," "game-changer," "world-class," "cutting-edge," "innovative solution," "next-level," "best-in-class" | "What does that actually look or feel like?" |
| Too generic | Could describe any brand in any category | "What makes that specifically *this* brand?" |
| Capability, not emotion | "superpowers," "empowered," "in control," "productive," "confident" | "Empowered to feel *what*? — more calm? elite? authoritative? energized?" Push the last rung to a real emotion; a capability is the vehicle, not the feeling. |
| Can't name the feeling | Stuck, circling, "I don't know how to say it" | Switch scaffolds: map the **arc** (chaotic→orderly? low-status→elite? stale→exciting?) or run **attribute spectrums** and *show* the poles (`attribute-spectrums.md`). Don't keep repeating the question. |
| Muse with no visual | An inspiration named for its mission, not its look | "What can we *steal* visually? If nothing, it's not a muse for us." |
| Muse captured flat | Cue recorded as a label or as "clean / minimal / modern" | "That's the summary, not the cue — what's the *texture, material, or dimension* we'd render? What would you want to touch?" |
| Tactical when emotional is needed | Features/logistics when asked about feeling | "Set the logistics aside — how should it *feel*?" |
| Contradiction / inconsistency | Answers that don't square — "elite" + "affordable"; an ideal user earning $500k who "shops at Aldi"; attribute words at war | Name it and clarify: "you said elite *and* affordable — which wins, and what does each actually mean to you?" Conflicts are signal (the founder may be wrong, or the words mean something specific); don't smooth them over. |
| Asks you for the answers | "Just suggest some muses / pick a direction for us" | Offer the deeper path first ("your own muses will fit better — want to try a few questions first?"); generate ideas only after they decline, framed as starting points to react to |

**Provoke, don't just capture.** Alongside the questions, bring strategy-grade angles from the brand's *own* material — the name's etymology/language, the category's unclaimed lane, the founder's truth — as provocations to react to (e.g. "Magister is the Latin title for the Roman Supreme Commander — lean into that?"). Critique what exists honestly and paint what it could become. This is not imposing your taste (which the emotional target must stay free of); it's the strategic thinking the client hired you for. ## 1. Vision & Truth
- What is this brand really for — beyond what it sells?
- What do you believe about this space that your competitors don't?
- If the product disappeared tomorrow, what would the world miss?
- **Mine the name yourself before the session:** what does it *mean* — etymology, language, literal translation, the thing it's named after? Bring any angle as a provocation ("your name literally means X — is there a whole identity in that?"). The strongest directions are often already hiding in the brand's own word.

## 2. The Ideal User
- Describe one real person this is for. Not a segment — a person.
- What do they already think is beautiful? Trustworthy? Premium? Fun?
- Where do they spend attention, and what does *that* look like?
- **Map their buying patterns, aesthetic priorities, and story arc** — the change they undergo (before → after). The arc lays the foundation for the visuals: a user going from *overwhelmed amateur → in-command* wants a different world than one going from *bored → delighted*.
- **Look for the founder-story parallel.** Founders often unknowingly build for a version of themselves — find where the founder's own journey mirrors the ideal user's arc (e.g. "I built bridges between teams" → a tool that unifies teams). It's rich material and it makes the brand ring true.

## 3. Competitive Visual Landscape
- Who are the 3–5 brands you're visually compared to?
- What colors, type, and imagery does the category default to?
- What would feel *wrong* or off-category here — and is that an opportunity?

## 4. Brand Personality
- If the brand walked into a room, who is it? Be specific.
- Pick three adjectives — now make each one specific. "Confident" *like whom*?
- What is the brand definitely *not*?

## 5. The Muses (spend the most time here — and SHOW them)
A muse is **not** a favorite or a thing to feature — it's a thing that makes *you* feel the target feeling. Always ask anchored to it: *"You want people to feel [X]. What makes *you* feel [X] — specifically, visually?"* If they answer with something they "love" or "want to show," check it: does it produce the feeling? If not, it's not a muse.
For every muse named: fetch real images and put them on screen before accepting it. Confirm against the image ("which part of this is the feeling?"), add it to the muse board, and record the image source in the working doc. A muse without a confirmed image isn't captured yet.
- *"What makes you feel [the target feeling]?"* — a film, a product, a place, an object, an interface, a material, another brand. (Not what you love; what produces the feeling.)
- For each: **what exactly would we steal — at physical fidelity?** Not the label ("Apollo Mission Control," "the OP-1") but the *texture, material, dimensionality, light, and imperfection*: the worn console panels, the machined knobs you want to press, the amber CRT glow, the graph-paper grain, the molded-plastic tactility. Record it at the level a designer must **render**, not decorate around. A cue captured as "clean" or "minimal" has been flattened before it left the room.
- Push past the obvious in-category muses. The best DNA usually comes from outside the category.
- **If they're stuck**, suggest varied options anchored to the feeling for them to react to — then dig into *why* whatever lands is the feeling. Their reaction selects; don't let your suggestion stand unexamined.

## 6. Emotional Target (Big Three)
- Of everything we've surfaced, what are the three feelings this brand must produce?
- For each: how would we *know* a design produced it?

**Getting to a real emotion (the whole job).** You are after a *feeling*, not a capability or a stance. When they answer with a capability ("superpowers," "empowered," "in control"), don't accept it — push the last rung. Tools, in order:
1. **Enter through the "before."** Ask *"what is their day / life like **before** your product?"* and offer concrete before-states (drowning in the backlog, faking expertise, outgunned by bigger teams). The feeling is the *release* from that before — name it vividly and the after-emotion falls out. Usually the fastest door.
2. **The "feel more ___" ladder.** Push the capability to the emotion it delivers: *feel more calm · at ease · creative · energized · tranquil · authoritative · elite · strong · peaceful · moody / introspective.* **These are not mutually exclusive** — elite/authoritative/calm can coexist; you're assembling a *blend* (the Big Three), not crowning one winner. Offer as "which are in the mix, how do they round out?", never "pick one."
3. **The full arc** — the shift the ideal user travels, led by the *from*: *drowning→running the room · faking it→credentialed authority · burned-out→effortless · stale→alive.* The shift names the feeling and how it shows up.
4. **Attribute spectrums** — place the brand on bipolar sliders (masculine↔feminine, natural↔industrial, refined↔sturdy, warm↔cool, playful↔serious…) and **show the poles** (or take a stab and let them shove it) rather than asking them to define words. The cluster of placements *is* the feeling in latent form. Full method: [attribute-spectrums.md](attribute-spectrums.md).

## 7. Fixed Core vs. Flexible Range
- What must *always* be present for this to be recognizably the brand?
- Where is the brand free to stretch — campaigns, premium tiers, seasonal, sub-brands?
- Imagine a piece that drops the everyday font and color entirely. What would still have to be true for it to count as on-brand?
