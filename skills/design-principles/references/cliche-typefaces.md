# Cliché Typefaces — the Catalog and the Law Behind It

The companion to [ai-slop.md](ai-slop.md)'s Type section. This is the field guide: which faces read as trite, *why*, and the principle that outlives any list.

## The wave law (read this before the list)

The most important finding from tracking this discourse is that **the ban list decays**. The 2024–25 "escape Inter" advice recommended Fraunces, Instrument Serif, Geist, Clash Display, and Satoshi as the *cure* for default-font slop — and within one cycle, the same design community was posting "Slop fonts: Space Grotesk, Instrument Serif, Geist, Syne, Fraunces." Yesterday's escape hatch is today's cliché. Lobster was the fresh alternative to Comic Sans; now "Lobster is the new Comic Sans." Montserrat was the indie darling; then Fiverr crowned it font of the year and it became wallpaper.

So the durable rule is not a list — it's a test:

> **A typeface is trite when it arrives without a decision.** If the face would appear anyway — because it's the template default, the model's statistical center, the current "designed-looking" fashion, or the literal costume for the muse — it is unchosen, and unchosen reads as slop regardless of the face's intrinsic quality.

Three failure modes, one law:
1. **The undecided default** — the face nobody picked (Inter because it's there, Arial because it shipped).
2. **The try-hard wave face** — the face picked *to look designed*, which is the same non-decision at one remove (whatever is currently "the tasteful choice" on every portfolio).
3. **The costume face** — the face picked because it literally matches the muse or category (Trajan for epic, Cinzel for Roman, Papyrus for ancient). An abstraction failure: you wore the muse instead of distilling it.

A face escapes the list only by being **the muse abstracted** (traceable, specific, ownable) or **a neutral chosen deliberately** so the system carries the distinction (see ai-slop.md — the sin is the absent decision, not neutrality).

## The catalog, by cohort

### 1. The AI / vibe-coding wave (the current tell — banned as defaults)
The faces LLMs and template kits reach for; their presence now *identifies* generated work.
- **Space Grotesk** — the #1 tell; "techy personality" grabbed for every AI/dev product
- **Fraunces** — the character-serif every generated "warm premium" brand lands on
- **Instrument Serif / Instrument Sans**
- **Syne**
- **Geist** — Vercel's system face; costume when worn outside Vercel's ecosystem
- The creator-template cohort: **DM Sans, Manrope, Plus Jakarta Sans, Outfit, Sora, Urbanist** — fine faces individually, but they arrive together as the "modern SaaS" kit, unchosen

### 2. The Fontshare / "premium free" wave (the next slop in line)
"General Sans + Satoshi shows up on half the well-designed SaaS sites"; "Clash Display + Satoshi dominates modern e-commerce." Quality faces mid-transition from alternative to wallpaper — treat as wave faces:
- **Satoshi**, **Clash Display**, **General Sans**, **Cabinet Grotesk**, **Switzer**

### 3. The undecided defaults (legitimate ONLY when deliberate)
- **Inter, Roboto, Arial, Helvetica-as-reflex, Open Sans**, raw system stacks
- The nuance stands: a neutral face *chosen* — with motif, color, composition, and treatment carrying the distinction — beats a try-hard face every time. The test is whether you can say why.

### 4. The startup-blanding era (2015–2020 Google Fonts homogenization)
Once-distinctive, now "evoke a tired trendiness rather than fresh innovation":
- **Montserrat** ("the craft beer of fonts — once niche, now absolutely everywhere"), **Poppins**, **Lato**, **Raleway**, **Nunito**

### 5. The Canva / DIY era
Defaults of drag-and-drop design; each signals its template:
- **Playfair Display** — the "luxury/elegant" default (weddings, coaches, every "premium" Canva kit); largely what Fraunces replaced, same trajectory
- **Bebas Neue** — the bold-condensed default for "impact"
- **Lobster** — "the new Comic Sans"; hipster café menus, Etsy logos
- **Dancing Script / Great Vibes** and the free-script shelf — "I picked the first free script font I could find"

### 6. Costume faces (category literalism — the abstraction failure)
- **Trajan** — "overused to the point of parody" on epic/historical movie posters (Titanic, GoT, A Beautiful Mind)
- **Cinzel** — web-font Trajan; all-caps Roman for anything imperial/classical
- **Papyrus** — "ancient/exotic/natural" costume (the Avatar meme)
- **Copperplate Gothic** — the law-firm / steakhouse / bank default; "rigid, lifeless"
- **Blackletter for medieval, Lithos for Greek**, and any face chosen because it *is* the muse rather than evoking it
- **Chop-suey / "wonton" faces** for anything East-Asian — cliché *and* offensive; never

### 7. The classic amateur tells (permanent list)
- **Comic Sans**, **Papyrus**, **Impact** (meme-ified), **Brush Script**, **Curlz MT**, **Bradley Hand**, **Hobo**, **Bleeding Cowboys**, **Jokerman**

## How to apply this

- **Cohorts 1–2 and 4–7: do not reach for these.** For cohorts 1–2 the ban is *as defaults/wave-picks* — a genuine muse-traced justification could theoretically earn one back, but the bar is "state why, or the default stands," and "it looks designed" is not a why.
- **Cohort 3 is conditional:** deliberate neutrality is a legitimate, often superior strategy (see the type-abstraction law in `creative-direction`).
- **Refresh the wave cohorts.** Anything topping "best free fonts" lists, Fontshare's most-downloaded, or every third portfolio *this year* is entering the wave. When auditing, ask: "would three other brands in this category plausibly ship this face this quarter?" If yes, it's not ownable.
- **The audit question is always the same:** was this face *decided* — and can the decision be stated in one sentence that traces to the muse or the emotional target?
