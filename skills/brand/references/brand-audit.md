# Brand Audit — The Nine Categories

This is the brand audit. Score the brand across nine categories (1–5), each grounded in the **confirmed competitor set** and the brand's emotional story. The output isn't a bare matrix — it's a per-category read a client can act on, and it drives the REO verdict in `brand` Step 2.

## Before you score

- Use the **confirmed** competitor set from Step 1 — distinctiveness is judged against it, *per category*, with named comparisons. **Source it from the marketing context, not a gut guess** (gut guesses are hit-or-miss): `.agents/product-marketing.md`'s Competitive Landscape if present, else the `competitor-profiling` skill. Score against the **direct** set; secondary/indirect players inform the landscape but aren't the visual peer group.
- Read the Emotional Target / brand story from `.agents/brand.md`. Several categories can't be judged without it (an Appropriate logo, on-brand graphics).
- **Creative Direction is the highest-leverage category and the usual weak link for differentiation — but it does NOT numerically cap the others.** Score each category on its own axis. Excellent craft is a 5 even when Creative Direction is only competent. (A real brand can score Creative Direction 3 and still earn 5s on Typography, Logo, Graphics, Icons, and Style Guide — that's a strong brand with a soft through-line, not a weak one.)
- **Test distinctiveness against the field; never assert it.** Don't rate a category strong on its own merits — score it *relative to the confirmed competitors*. A clean logo is not "distinct" until you've laid it beside the competitor marks; a consistent color is not "owned" until you've checked no competitor shares it.
- **Don't reward the cluster.** A high rank in a tightly-clustered category is *not* distinctiveness (see `design-principles` → Coherent Is Not the Same as Good) — if everyone looks alike, "#2 in the category" means generic, not strong.
- **Don't let execution-drift fool you into Optimization.** Finding stale assets, half-finished migrations, or fragmenting color does *not* by itself mean the brand is sound-but-poorly-executed. Execution drift routinely sits *on top of* a weak, undifferentiated foundation. Score the foundation's distinctiveness honestly and independently *first*, then judge depth.
- **Different is not directed.** A brand can be a clear visual outlier in its category (low competitor similarity) and *still* score low on Creative Direction — being unlike the field is not the same as being anchored in an intentional, muse-derived through-line. Don't let low similarity inflate the Creative Direction (or any foundational) score.
- **Score what's deployed, not the potential.** Rate the brand as it actually is — not what it could be if the assets were used well. A differentiated color that's barely used (a neutral dominates), or a distinctive mark that lives only in the favicon, is a **low** score with a note that there's latent equity to build on. "It could be distinctive if deployed" goes in *Moving forward*, never the score. (Latent, undeployed equity is often what makes a brand an Evolution.)
- **Implementation flaws are not weak assets.** Look *through* a rough site. A distinctive pattern at blown-out contrast is a *strong asset, badly executed* (an Optimization signal), not a weak pattern; a great typeface buried in banded layouts is still a great typeface. Assess each asset's intrinsic quality *separately* from how well the current site implements it — a strong, distinct, **deployed** asset that's just mis-tuned scores on its merits, and the execution becomes the work. (Undeployed → low, build it out; deployed-but-mis-executed → strong asset, fix the build.)

## Output format — present it in full

This breakdown is the **deliverable** — present it to the user in the response, in full. Never collapse it into a one-line verdict or only save it to a file. Lead with a **scorecard table** (all nine categories + scores + a blank "Your score" column + the competitor-similarity read + verdict/path), then the per-category breakdown below, then the competitor set, name flag, and AI-slop findings.

For each category: a **Score /5**, then three short sections:
- **Why this matters** — the leverage this category has.
- **Current status** — what's actually there, with *named* competitor comparisons (who it resembles or collides with).
- **Moving forward** — the specific path; the foundational categories usually route back to `brand-strategy` (the Blueprint).

## Scoring anchors

Two scales, because the two kinds of category are judged on different axes (see below):

**Foundational categories — distinctiveness axis:**
`1` = fails / generic default / competitor collision · `2` = clean but undifferentiated · `3` = competent, some ownable signal · `4` = distinct · `5` = distinct, ownable, well-executed.

**Craft categories — execution axis:**
`1` = broken / incohesive / crude · `2` = inconsistent · `3` = competent, unremarkable · `4` = strong, cohesive execution · `5` = exceptional, fully consistent execution. **A common typeface, a stock icon style, or an undistinctive-but-flawless system can be a 5** — distinctiveness is upside noted in *Moving forward*, never a penalty here. But the execution axis cuts **both ways**: judge the actual work — hierarchy, spacing, readability, whether marketing and product UI blur together — not the *names* of the typefaces or tools. Real flaws (oversized unreadable headlines, cramped edges, inconsistent weights, a dated feel, screenshots bleeding into the brand layer) pull a craft score to 2 even with a respectable typeface. **Clean ≠ well-executed** (see `design-principles`): a minimal, consistent-at-a-glance surface is not automatically a 3–4. Inspect for the flaws generic SaaS hides — no type scale, inconsistent heading weights, right-aligned/ragged body copy, unpredictable grid/spacing, mixed icon styles (emoji next to line icons), non-concentric radii. A tidy-looking page carrying several of these is a **2**, not a 3.

(AI-slop tropes cap a category at 2, both types — see `design-principles` → [ai-slop.md](../../design-principles/references/ai-slop.md).)

## Two kinds of category — score them on different axes

The nine split into two groups, and applying one lens to both is the most common scoring error:

- **Foundational / distinctiveness** — Creative Direction, Color, Logo, Pattern & Texture. Judged *hard against the field* on ownership and distinctiveness. Generic or competitor-colliding here = low, however clean. **These drive the REO verdict.**
- **Craft / consistency / system** — Typography & Layout, Cohesive Shapes & Graphics, Icon Libraries, Application Across Assets, Style Guide. Judged on *execution quality, cohesion, and system discipline*. **Give genuine credit for well-made, consistent work even when it isn't distinct** — a strong-but-generic execution is a 4, and an *exceptional, fully cohesive* one is a **5**. Creative Direction does **not** cap these. A 1–2 here means the craft or consistency is genuinely poor (mismatched type across assets, broken nesting, no system) — *not* merely that it lacks a distinct point of view or a common typeface.

Don't apply the anti-generic / anti-slop lens uniformly. "Generic" rightly tanks a foundational category; it must not zero out a craft category that's competently and consistently executed.

## The nine categories

### 1. Distinct Creative Direction — the keystone *(foundational)*
Is there an emotional through-line *stolen from real reference points* (muses) and translated into a visual language — or just consistency? **Consistency is not direction. And neither is mere difference** — a brand can be a warm, mascot-led outlier in a cool-minimal category (low competitor similarity) and *still* score 1 here, because the difference isn't anchored in a deliberate feeling. Score this on whether there's an intentional, muse-derived through-line, **independent of how unlike the field it looks.** A brand with *no* consistent or differentiated identity — a pure template, thinly deployed — is a **1**. But a **consistently-deployed, somewhat-differentiated element (an owned-ish color used consistently, a product-line vocabulary, a mature and coherent presence) can reach 2–3 even with no deliberate muse.** Don't auto-1 a generic-but-consistent brand; reserve 1 for genuinely no identity. *(Generic and thinly deployed → 1. Generic but with a consistently-applied, differentiated-in-set color and a branded product vocabulary → 3.)* The 1–3 range here is real judgment — don't chase a single number. This is the highest-leverage category and the usual ceiling on *distinctiveness* — but it does not numerically cap the other categories' scores; a brand can have excellent craft and a soft direction. Keep it separate from **competitor similarity** (the *comparative* visual-overlap read below): different ≠ directed, and looking like the category ≠ having no through-line.

### 2. Typography & Layout
The structural skeleton. Cover **both halves**. *Type:* hierarchy, bold/secondary use, and — critically — **consistency across assets** (a deck face whose letterforms differ from the site, e.g. a different "A", is a system failure). Typeface as a differentiator: in a category of near-identical sans, a distinctive face is high-leverage. *Layout:* grid discipline, section rhythm (are near-identical blocks stacked with no variation or eye-leading?), and whether *internal* pages (About, etc.) hold the homepage's standard or fall off. Clean, consistent execution = 3–4 even if the face is generic; reserve 1–2 for genuine inconsistency (mismatched faces asset-to-asset, no hierarchy in the deck).

### 3. Color
An owned territory, or the category default? **Verify ownership — don't assert it.** A color is "owned" only if no direct competitor occupies the same territory; **name the competitors sharing the palette**. A green that three direct rivals already run is the category default, not an owned color, however consistently the brand applies it — and "we're diluting our color" is the wrong read when the color was never owned to begin with. Check the CTA color is distinct from supporting graphics (functional hierarchy, not just brand). Watch for a competitor having already claimed the obvious conceptual color territory implied by the name (e.g. a bigger rival already owning the spectrum narrative implied by your own name).

### 4. Pattern & Texture
A passive cohesion tool that signals the brand without a logo or even color — invaluable for a brand with a light sales motion. Is there a distinct motif, deployed across collateral — or one that **collides with a competitor** (e.g. overlapping a direct rival's mark or signature texture)? Is it used off-site at all? **Look before you say "none"** — a deployed-but-imperfect motif (e.g. a dot pattern with alignment issues, applied broadly but not pushed to its potential) is a **3**, not a zero; confirm visually that no pattern exists before scoring it absent. A lone decorative grid with no rationale or recurrence is a 1. The most brand-coherent pattern source is usually the **logo's own geometry** — check whether the mark's shapes have been developed into a system, and name the opportunity if not (e.g. two trapezoids forming a hexagon).

### 5. Cohesive Shapes & Graphics
The brand's visual vocabulary — branded abstractions, motifs, graphic metaphors, illustration-level work. A **craft/consistency** category: assess execution, not just distinctiveness. Do inner elements nest concentrically (inner radius = outer radius − padding)? Are data-visualizations treated in line with the brand's softness (curved vs jagged lines, consistent fills)? Are effects (glass, shadow, gradient) applied as a *system* or as one-offs? Do the deck's graphics read as the same brand as the site? **Boxed product screenshots + miscellaneous pills/frames are not a cohesive branded system** — that's a 2, not a 3; genuine cohesion means branded *abstractions* of the product with a unified shape/treatment language. Well-made, genuinely cohesive graphics earn a 3–4 even if generic-to-category; reserve 1–2 for incohesive, crude, or screenshot-dump work. Distinctiveness (motifs specific to *this* brand's story) moves a strong 4 to a 5.

### 6. Logo — SAD
Run `logo-design`. **First, actually find the mark** — inspect the favicon, app icon, and full lockup (not just the nav) for a **symbol/glyph**; don't conclude "wordmark-only, no mark" from the nav alone, and don't penalize a wordmark for being a wordmark if it's genuinely distinct in the set. **Simple** (a single source of truth — one consistent construction, consistent light/shadow/orientation across instances; holds in black & white to a favicon — check *multiple* real instances, not one clean nav SVG), **Appropriate** (carries the story — needs the Emotional Target to judge), **Distinct** (*actually lay the mark beside the competitor logos* and compare silhouettes against high-profile marks — flag collisions like a mark nearly identical in silhouette to a high-profile tech brand's). **SAD is not averaged: failing one criterion sinks the score — but distinguish *fatal* failures from *fixable* ones.** Fatal (sink to ≤2): a competitor-silhouette collision, illegibility at required sizes, a mark that misreads as something else. Fixable-execution issues — a gradient that should be flattened, a stale app-icon crop, a metallic effect fighting a flat system — are *point-docks and Phase-1 fixes*, not fatal: a sound mark that only needs its gradient killed is still a **3**, not a 2. Don't sink a workable logo for a one-line fix. A Simple, attractive mark that *collides* with a competitor's silhouette, though, is a *failing* logo — don't let a B&W-legible nav SVG talk you into calling it a category strength. A **recognition failure** also caps it: a mark that misreads as something else at small sizes (a mascot that reads as a different animal in the favicon) fails Simple even if it's otherwise distinct — flag it and let it move the score, don't note it and move on. And **don't inflate a wordmark via best-in-a-weak-set**: "more distinctive than the competitors' wordmarks" is the cluster trap at the logo level. A functional wordmark with no distinct symbol/mark, not anchored to a creative direction, is a **2** even if rivals are also plain wordmarks — being the least-plain of a plain set is not distinctiveness. Real logo equity needs a mark that's ownable in absolute terms, not just relative to a weak field. **Construction flaws cap it too:** awkward kerning/letter-spacing (an ungainly "E–V" gap), unbalanced internal whitespace, geometry that reads as unintentional (a rotation with no rationale), or no small-size/secondary lockup. A clever *concept* doesn't rescue a mark whose construction is sloppy — that's a 2, not a 3. Assess the **wordmark/lockup**, not only the mark glyph: inconsistent weight or size between words (e.g. the first word bold, the second light) adds cognitive load and can imply an unintended sub-brand — a consistency issue even when the glyph is fine.

### 7. Defined Icon Libraries — three tiers
A **craft** category. A clean, consistent, stylistically unified icon set is a strong **4 on execution — even a stock/Material/library set** (consistent + cohesive is what's being scored; "stock" is *not* a penalty here). Push it stylistically to reach 5. The three-tier model — **utility** (functional UI), **storytelling** (narrative), **illustration** (personality) — is an *opportunity to grow into* (note it in *Moving forward*), not a requirement that caps the score. Reserve low scores for genuinely mixed, inconsistent, or crude icon styles (multiple clashing stroke weights/fills, a "childish" set fighting the rest). *(Don't dock a consistent set to a 3 for being "stock" — that's the mistake to avoid.)*

### 8. Application Across Assets
The website is the most controlled environment a brand has; the real test is everywhere else — thumbnails, ads, social, decks, content. Are there **portable brand tools** (background patterns, approved motifs, template structures, explicit rules) so off-site work stays on-brand — or does it fragment (one-off typefaces, stray motifs, a drop shadow that appears in exactly one place)? Judge against the off-site assets gathered in Step 1. **Presence is not strength.** Social channels that merely *exist* and are internally consistent are not a 4 — this category rewards *substantial, varied, on-brand output across many surfaces*. Thin or sparse off-site expression (a handful of posts, little ad/email/deck presence) is a 2, even if what little exists is consistent. **Near-absent off-site — a single channel, no OG-art system, no templates, no portable tools — is a 1:** the brand barely exists once it leaves the website.

### 9. Style Guide
Is there an operating guide a new contributor could produce on-brand work from? **It need not be a published document** — an internally consistent *marketing/brand* system implies an embedded guide, and that counts. **But a product/app-UI style guide or a web design system / component library is NOT a brand style guide** — in-app design tokens, Webflow CSS variables, a shadcn/component system don't count toward this score. And **an implicit system only implies a guide if it's genuinely *adhered to*** — a web system with visible **drift** (competing color values, anomalies, legacy-font leftovers, a stale icon) is proof there's *no working guide*, so it's **0–1**, not partial credit for "a system exists." Adherence, not existence, earns the points. If there's no brand/marketing guide (explicit or implied by genuinely coherent marketing), it's **0–1**. *(e.g. a coherent-looking web system undermined by five competing blues, font drift, and a stale icon — no working guide → 0.)* Score on three things: **existence** (a standalone guide *or* an implicit-but-coherent system — don't score 1 just because there's no `/brand` page), **adherence** (do the assets actually follow it — the deck using a different logo lockup than the site is an adherence failure), and **coverage** (does it span all asset types and explain *why*, or only the web?). A coherent web system that isn't enforced across the deck/off-site and has no standalone artifact is ~3, not 1. A published spec sheet nobody follows is barely better than nothing. The best guides explain *why*, not just *what*.

## Beyond the nine — the name (verbal identity)

The nine categories are visual; the **name** is brand equity too, and a taxing one is real friction. Flag it separately (it isn't a 1–5 visual score):

- **The radio test** — if someone *hears* the name, can they reliably spell it and find it? Coined or respelled words (e.g. a respelled portmanteau) fail this: ambiguous spelling hurts word-of-mouth, search, and recall.
- **The mouth test** — is it easy to *say*? Count syllables, awkward consonant clusters, and ambiguous stress. A name that's phonetically or linguistically taxing on the mouth and the mind taxes every customer who tries to refer you.

Note the issue in the audit and in `.agents/brand.md`. A rename is a heavy, separate decision — if it's on the table, that's `brand-naming`, not this audit. But surfacing the friction is part of an honest brand read.

## Competitor similarity — the pivot (weighted heaviest)

A measure distinct from Creative Direction, and the single biggest driver of the recommended path. Creative Direction is the *generative* question (is there an emotional through-line?). **Competitor similarity is the *comparative* question: with the logo covered, could a customer tell this brand apart from its direct competitors on the foundational visual elements — color, graphic/image treatment, typography, logo silhouette, overall aesthetic?** A brand can have a soft through-line yet still look distinct enough, or a clear one yet still look like the category — so assess this on its own and weight it more than any single category.

Rate it explicitly:
- **High / indistinguishable** — foundational elements match named rivals (color identical to two rivals; graphics and dark-mode treatment like the rest of the field).
- **Moderate** — distinct enough, some crossover, not remarkably its own (recognizable but in the neighborhood — solid color, soft direction).
- **Low** — unmistakably its own; distinctiveness isn't the constraint.

## Driving the REO verdict — a phased recommendation, anchored to appetite

**The verdict is not a single label derived from the scores. It's the recommended *work* — anchored to the client's appetite and timeline and to what's actionable now — and it is often *phased*.** A brand can score low across the board and still be **"Optimization now, deeper work later."** (A real pattern: a brand scoring 17/45, genuinely generic — yet the right recommendation was *Optimization now* because the client wanted to move incrementally, with creative direction as a later macro phase.)

So the order of operations is:

1. **Ask about appetite and timeline first** — how much do they want to change *now* vs. later; incremental polish or a transformational break? This isn't visible in the assets and it's the *primary* driver of the recommendation. **Never override a client's incremental appetite with a diagnosis-driven verdict, and never argue against the depth they've asked for.**
2. **Diagnose honestly** (competitor similarity + the scores) to shape the *roadmap* — not to pick one label and reject the rest.
3. **Recommend a path, usually sequenced:**
   - **Optimization** — the immediate design-fundamentals work on the *existing* assets: typography, hierarchy, contrast, layout, palette rationalization, consistency. Valid as the **starting point for any brand whose owner wants to move incrementally — even a generic-foundation one** — with the deeper work teed up for later. Also the terminal answer when the assets are strong and only execution lags (the strong-assets-badly-built case).
   - **Evolution** — keep the equity; do generative build-out (creative direction, motif, deploy missing pieces) — the equity-in-a-drawer case.
   - **Revolution** — rebuild the foundation: colliding/undifferentiated with little to keep (the lookalike case), or the client wants a transformational break and has the appetite for it.

**Be honest about what each phase buys.** Optimization-now makes a generic brand *tidier and better-executed* — not *distinct*; distinctiveness comes from the creative-direction phase (Evolution/Revolution). Say that plainly: don't sell polish as distinctiveness, and don't *refuse* polish because distinctiveness needs more. The common, correct shape is **"micro now (Optimization) → macro later (creative direction)."**

The competitor-similarity + appetite reads below shape *which* deeper path the roadmap heads toward:

**Distinctiveness is a step-change, not a dial you nudge.** You cannot incrementally move a brand from "distinct enough" to "remarkably distinct" — closing real competitor similarity is a wholesale shift, not a refinement.

- **High similarity (indistinguishable on the foundationals)** → incremental polish cannot *create distinction*. If the client wants to stand out, the deeper path is **Revolution**. Optimization-now can still be a legitimate first phase (tidy the fundamentals while the rebuild is scoped), but be explicit it won't deliver distinctiveness — that comes from the Revolution. Don't sell polish as the fix; don't refuse it as a first step either. *(The lookalike case: the client wanted to look new fast, so Revolution led.)*
- **Moderate similarity (distinct enough, crossover present, craft strong)** → a genuine **fork, and it is the client's strategic choice**, not a default:
  - **Optimization** — if they're content with the current level of overlap and just tired of the brand: keep the established assets, execute them at a higher level and in more interesting ways, and shore up the gaps the audit found. No rebuild.
  - **Revolution** — if they want to actually break away and become remarkably distinct: a wholesale shift (new emotional direction, new visual language), *not* an incremental tweak. Say plainly that you can't nudge the existing system there.
  *(Strong craft, soft direction, moderate overlap → present both; recommend per their appetite.)*

  **Never foreclose Revolution when the foundation is generic.** Even when Evolution is the sensible recommendation (real equity in the name/concept), a generic foundation at moderate–high similarity means Revolution is the legitimate *appetite-up* option — present it as "recommended if the team is open to it," don't declare it "not warranted" just because some equity exists. The keepable equity (a good name, a differentiated concept) usually survives a Revolution anyway; what gets rebuilt is the visual language. *(e.g. Evolution at minimum, full Revolution recommended if the team is open to it.)*
- **Low similarity** → distinctiveness isn't the constraint; now read *why the work looks like it does*:
  - **Strong, distinct assets that exist and are deployed, but are implemented poorly** — pattern at blown-out contrast, weak/banded layouts, typography underused → **Optimization**: keep every asset, fix the *build*. *(Great color, pattern, logo, typefaces — badly executed on the site.)*
  - **Strong, distinct, well-executed, deployed** → **Optimization** as well: push and systematize what's already working.
  - **Difference that's latent or undeployed** — a differentiated color barely used, a mark stuck in the favicon, no through-line anchoring the outlier look → **Evolution**: the raw equity is real but isn't working yet; anchor it (Creative Direction) and build it out, don't start over. *(The "equity in a drawer" case.)*

**Optimization vs Evolution** (both keep the equity): Optimization changes only *how existing strong, distinct assets are implemented* — pure execution. Evolution also *creates, defines, or deploys* pieces that are missing, unanchored, or undeployed — generative work on top of the keepers.

Weigh the client's stated ambition. Always present the **recommended path with rationale, plus the alternative and its tradeoff** — and in the moderate case, frame the fork explicitly as their call on appetite, not yours.

## Optional: the ranked matrix

For a fast distinctiveness read you can score the brand and each confirmed competitor across these categories in a matrix and rank them — useful to show where the brand clusters with the field. The per-category audit above is the deliverable; the matrix is a summary.
